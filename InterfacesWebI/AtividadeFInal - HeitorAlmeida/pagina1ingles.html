What is Urban Mobility?

Urban mobility is the condition of moving from one point to another within a city.

This is the most commonly found definition, but also the most simplistic, reductionist, and literal one - as "mobility" refers to the ability to move, and "urban" refers to the city.

Currently, the term urban mobility also refers to an area of knowledge and public management that takes into account a "need to be satisfied."

This is what Diego Hernández, an expert in public transportation and urban mobility and a professor at the Catholic University of Uruguay, draws attention to in a video produced by the Development Bank of Latin America (CAF).

According to Hernández, urban mobility is a system that exists to satisfy people's transportation needs.

For this to happen, the work requires investments in infrastructure, good traffic management, and safety.

In addition, it involves the diversification of transportation modes - road, waterway, and rail (underground or surface) - and their integration.

Finally, it is worth noting that urban mobility is not limited to travel time.

For example, traveling from one place to another by bicycle can be faster than by car (due to the ability to bypass congestion).

However, without a bike lane or proper signage, cyclists will not feel safe to take the route.

Urban Mobility in Brazil

The Federal Constitution of Brazil states that road safety encompasses certain actions that "guarantee citizens the right to efficient urban mobility" (Article 144, paragraph 10, item I).

This function is attributed to traffic agencies.

The problem is that in the country, the notion of "traffic" is very limited to the practices of organizing the flow of motor vehicles.

Traffic agencies are mainly concerned with the safety of drivers, passengers, cyclists, and pedestrians.

This is undeniably a fundamental issue, but urban mobility is more than that, as we explained before.

With this reductionist view, what ends up happening is that, for the government, urban mobility actions are restricted to road infrastructure projects.

Investments in public transportation, on the other hand, are often disconnected from this infrastructure planning.

Urban Mobility Law

The major problem with the mindset we have just discussed is the lack of understanding that all these issues need to be interconnected within a broader planning framework.

Urban mobility is infrastructure, it is public transportation, and more. However, all of this needs to be considered together.

A federal law passed in 2012 aims to address this problem: Law No. 12,587, which establishes the guidelines for the National Urban Mobility Policy.

This law determined that all municipalities in the country with more than 20,000 inhabitants needed to develop an Urban Mobility Plan and align it with the municipal master plan within six years from the law's enactment.

According to Article 24, the plan must encompass:

  • Public collective transportation services
  • Traffic circulation
  • Urban mobility system infrastructure
  • Urban mobility system infrastructure, including bike lanes and paths
  • Accessibility for people with disabilities and mobility restrictions
  • Integration of public transportation modes and their integration with private and non-motorized modes
  • Operation and regulation of cargo transportation in the road infrastructure
  • Trip-generating areas
  • Public and private parking areas, whether free or paid
  • Restricted or controlled access and circulation areas and schedules
  • Mechanisms and instruments for financing public collective transportation and urban mobility infrastructure
  • Systematic evaluation, review, and periodic updating of the Urban Mobility Plan within a period not exceeding ten years.

The final deadline for plan development was three years after the law's enactment, which would have been in 2015. However, a law published in 2018 extended this deadline to 2019.

Municipalities that fail to comply with this requirement are prohibited from receiving federal budgetary resources allocated to urban mobility.

Challenges Faced by the City

The city of São Paulo faces various challenges related to urban mobility, due to its size, population density, and unplanned growth over the years. Some of the main challenges include:

  1. Traffic congestion: São Paulo has a high volume of vehicles circulating daily, resulting in constant traffic congestion on major city roads. Heavy traffic causes delays, stress, and negative impacts on air quality.
  2. Insufficient public transportation: Although São Paulo has an extensive public transportation network, including buses, subway, and trains, the demand is high, and often the supply is insufficient. This results in overcrowding, long waits, and discomfort for users.
  3. Lack of integration between transportation modes: The lack of efficient integration between different modes of public transportation hinders citizens' mobility. The lack of connectivity between buses, subway, and trains makes it difficult to make trips with modal transfers, leading to longer journeys.
  4. Inadequate infrastructure for cyclists and pedestrians: The city faces challenges in terms of infrastructure for cyclists and pedestrians. The lack of safe and well-signaled bike lanes makes it difficult to use bicycles as a means of transportation. Additionally, inadequate sidewalks and lack of accessibility compromise pedestrian mobility.
  5. Traffic safety: São Paulo has high rates of traffic accidents, including pedestrian accidents and collisions. Lack of respect for traffic rules, recklessness, and inadequate infrastructure contribute to insecurity on the city's roads.
  6. Inequality in the distribution of transportation services: Some more distant and peripheral neighborhoods have fewer options for public transportation, making it difficult for these communities to access essential services such as healthcare, education, and employment.
  7. Pollution and environmental impacts: The intense traffic of vehicles contributes to air pollution, which negatively affects public health. In addition, greenhouse gas emissions from the transportation sector contribute to climate change.

To address these challenges, the city of São Paulo has been implementing measures such as expanding the public transportation network, creating exclusive bus lanes, expanding the cycling infrastructure, adopting bike-sharing programs, and implementing traffic restrictions like vehicle rotation. Furthermore, promoting sustainable mobility solutions, such as incentivizing the use of electric vehicles, has also been discussed and implemented in the city.
